00 / OVERVIEW

From first principles to parts on the car.

I led the aerodynamic development of Triton Racing's TR26 car across the full engineering pipeline: setting the design philosophy, selecting and optimizing the airfoils, running the full-car CFD, validating trends in the wind tunnel, designing the composite structures, and manufacturing the parts.

I'm continuing with the team as both Aerodynamics Lead and Vehicle Dynamics Lead for the 2026–27 season, owning the aero package and the vehicle-dynamics work it feeds into.

The package produces 179 N of downforce against 131 N of drag at the 15 m/s competition-average speed (CL = 2.72, CD = 1.99, L/D = 1.37), with a 59.5% front aerodynamic balance. Every headline number is backed by a grid-independence study, a force-monitor convergence assessment, and an independent wind-tunnel check on the trends. All simulation was performed in ANSYS Fluent 2025 R2; structures were analyzed in FEA and validated on custom test rigs; parts were built in-house from carbon fiber.

Context: the team had not completed an aerodynamics package in the previous two seasons. The brief was deliberately disciplined, establish the fundamentals, keep the package simple enough to finish and manufacture well, and do it for under $2,000, engineering understanding over complexity.

01 / DESIGN & 2D OPTIMIZATION

Choosing and tuning the wings.

Each wing started as an airfoil-selection problem, then a 2D CFD optimization to squeeze the most downforce out of the allowed envelope before committing to the full car.

The wings were selected for high lift that holds up in the dirty, low-Reynolds air of an FSAE car (chord Reynolds number ≈ 5×10⁵, Ncrit = 5). The front wing is a two-element design, a NACA 4412 main element (300 mm chord, 5° AoA) and a Selig S1223 high-lift flap (180 mm chord, ~25° AoA). The rear wing is a three-element stack, an Eppler 423 main and flap (480 mm and 200 mm) topped by a Selig S1223 (125 mm), with the slot gaps tuned for attached flow over the whole stack.

The optimization was a real design-of-experiments study, not hand-tuning. Wing chords, angles of attack, and slot gaps were swept across dozens of CFD design points to fit a response-surface metamodel, which was then searched for the configuration that maximized downforce inside the rules envelope. The front wing reached a 2D lift coefficient of 2.49; the optimized rear wing reached a 3D CL of −3.93 at CD 0.118. The 2D wing cases were run wall-resolved (y⁺ ≈ 1, 14 inflation layers, 0.021 mm first cell) so the boundary layer and any separation were captured directly.

A lesson banked for next year: the parametric study should use more than 50 points, and blunted trailing edges in 2D pushed the predicted optimum past the real stall angle, both now documented for the next design cycle.
02 / FULL-CAR CFD

The whole car, not just the wings.

Wings designed in isolation behave differently once they sit behind a chassis, wheels, and a roll-hoop wake. A full-car RANS study quantified the real package and where its load actually comes from.

The baseline ran at 15 m/s with a moving ground plane and rotating wheels to reproduce true on-track flow, using the GEKO (k-ω generalized) turbulence model on a 7-million-cell poly-hexcore mesh with y⁺ ≈ 50 wall treatment. A three-level grid-independence study (3.3 / 4.8 / 7.0 M cells) following Celik et al. confirmed the result is a property of the physics, not the mesh: lift changed under 1% between the medium and fine meshes and the fine-mesh GCI was 0.93%.

Resolving the vertical force by component showed the rear wing (−95 N) and front wing (−82 N) do almost all the work, the chassis is vertically near-neutral while carrying roughly three-quarters of the drag, and the exposed wheels nearly cancel. The single most actionable finding came from the wake: because the roll-hoop wake convects into the rear wing, the rear wing under-performs relative to its planform, pointing at cleaning up that wake as the clearest future gain.

03 / OPERATING ENVELOPE

How the car behaves on track.

A car never sits at one attitude. The package was swept across speed, pitch, heave, yaw, and roll, and a dedicated rotating-frame skidpad case captured true cornering, building a sensitivity map the vehicle-dynamics group can interpolate.

Pitch is by far the strongest sensitivity: nose-down braking loads the front wing and the underbody hard, swinging the aerodynamic balance by more than 20 points across the usable range. That migration, balance is not a fixed number, is exactly the kind of result the vehicle-dynamics group has to design the car around. The speed sweep's Reynolds-independence result is also what makes the reduced-scale wind-tunnel comparison defensible later.

04 / WIND-TUNNEL VALIDATION

An independent physical check.

A 9% scale 3D-printed model was tested in the UC San Diego wind tunnel, deliberately not a one-to-one CFD match, but an honest check on whether the predicted trends show up in physical measurements.

Two signatures the CFD predicted both showed up in the physical test: aerodynamic load scaling with dynamic pressure (R² > 0.996), and a strong pitch dependence of the normal force (−12.7 N at braking pitch to −3.9 N at acceleration pitch), in the same direction as the CFD pitch sweep. To make a direct comparison, I ran one CFD case at the tunnel's exact proportions with a static ground. The dimensionless normal-to-axial force ratio matched to 3.8% (0.808 CFD vs 0.778 tunnel), meaning the simulation captures the shape of the load correctly. The absolute coefficients differ ~42%, entirely in the direction the known tunnel limitations predict: the uncorrected 4.8% blockage and the visibly deflecting model both inflate the measured force. So the experiment validates the trend; the magnitude gap is a property of the experiment, not a CFD error.

05 / STRUCTURES & FEA

Making the load path real.

Downforce only helps if the wings survive it and stay rigid. The composite spars and aluminum mounts were sized in FEA against conservative load cases, then checked on physical test rigs.

The rear-wing spar is a closed-section carbon box beam (Hexcel AS4 2×2 twill, US Composites 635 epoxy) carrying the ~400 N distributed download on six supports, designed to the Puck composite-failure criterion with a deflection limit of 10 mm. The mounts were topology-optimized in 6061-T6 aluminum against a conservative 1,500 N case, iterated alongside the evolving wing geometry, with every bolt checked for net-section and shear-out failure. I also built static-deflection rigs to validate the FEA against measured data and, just as usefully, to learn where the idealized fixed-support models were too stiff.

06 / MANUFACTURING

Building it in-house.

The package was designed to be manufacturable on the team's timeline and budget, and then actually built, from foam cores and molds to a quantified vacuum-bagging process.

I chose vacuum wet lay-up over prepreg for the whole 2026 package: prepreg's strength-to-weight edge is unnecessary for FSAE aero loads, and its long, mold-and-autoclave-intensive cure cycle is incompatible with the build timeline. Wing elements were built over hot-wire-cut foam cores (airfoil profiles exported from SolidWorks as DXF, offset inward by the skin thickness) sandwiched in wet carbon, and the front-wing skins were laid in CNC-milled molds.

To make the process decision quantitative rather than anecdotal, I ran a controlled experiment: 20 coupons, 10 vacuum-bagged against 10 cured at atmospheric pressure, measuring resin uptake, final weight, bond-line thickness, and fiber volume fraction. Vacuum bagging raised the fiber volume fraction from 0.47 to 0.55, cut added resin by 58%, and produced lower-variance (more repeatable) parts.

07 / INTEGRATION & OUTCOME

A package that earns its weight.

The aero work was closed back into the vehicle as a system. A mass and moment-of-inertia study (weighing each component and updating the CAD) quantified the rotational-inertia penalty against the grip gain: the package raises the lateral force available per tire by ~13% at the front and ~9% at the rear. A ground-clearance study cross-referenced the wing's lowest point against the full vehicle-dynamics motion envelope, pitch, roll, and heave, down to the 2.0 g braking case, and confirmed no floor strike anywhere in the envelope.

Across the whole program, the through-line is that every claim is defended: grid independence and GCI on the CFD, force-monitor convergence rather than residuals alone, an independent wind-tunnel trend check, conservative FEA with rig validation, and a manufacturing process backed by a designed experiment. The honest limitations, RAM-capped mesh refinement, uncorrected tunnel blockage, idealized FEA supports, are documented alongside the results, with a clear path to closing each one.
